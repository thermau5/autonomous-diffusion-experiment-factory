\section{Theory}
\label{sec:theory}

\paragraph{Setup.} Let \(p_0=\mathcal N(0,I)\) be the prior and
\(p_1 = p_{\mathrm{data}}\) the data distribution. The generative ODE
in noise coordinate \(\sigma\) is
\[
\frac{dx}{d\sigma} = g\bigl(x,\sigma;\theta\bigr),
\qquad
g(x,\sigma;\theta) = \frac{x - D_\theta(x,\sigma)}{\sigma},
\]
with \(D_\theta\) the pretrained EDM denoiser. A \emph{sampler} is the discretized controlled flow
\[
x_{i+1} = F_s\bigl(x_i,\sigma_i,\sigma_{i+1};\theta,\mathcal H_i\bigr),
\quad i = 0,\ldots,K-1,
\]
specified by the tuple
\[
u = (\theta,\ \pi,\ s,\ m,\ N)
\]
where \(\theta\) is the backbone, \(\pi\) is the path/coupling family, \(s\)
the solver core (update law \(F_s\)), \(m(\sigma)\) the sampling-grid
density inducing \(\sigma_0>\sigma_1>\cdots>\sigma_K=0\), and \(N\)
the cost budget (NFE / wall-clock).

\paragraph{Schedule equivalence under coordinate change.} The names
\emph{noise schedule}, \emph{time schedule}, and \emph{grid schedule}
refer to the same mathematical object \(m\) viewed in different
coordinates. A noise schedule specifies \(\sigma(t)\) along a continuous
time axis; the inverse \(t(\sigma)\) is the time schedule on the noise
axis; and the discrete grid \(\{\sigma_i\}_{i=0}^{K}\) is the
quantile sampling of either, related to \(m\) by inverse-CDF
\(\sigma_i = M^{-1}(i/K)\) where \(M(\sigma)=\int_\sigma^B m(s)\,ds\).
Under any smooth monotone reparameterization
\(\varphi:\sigma\mapsto\sigma'\), the certificate's two integrals
transform covariantly --- \(m\), \(d_s\), and \(a\) all transform as
densities --- and the closed-form optimizer
\(m^\star_s(\sigma)\propto d_s(\sigma)^{1/(p+1)}\) is invariant up to
the same change of variable. So Karras's \(\rho\)-parameterized noise
schedule, the DDPM \(\beta\)-schedule on time, and our discrete grid
\(\{\sigma_i\}\) are equivalent control objects; the theory and the
implementation can be stated in whichever coordinate is most
convenient for a given solver. We use \(\sigma\)-density throughout
because the EDM net is naturally parameterized in \(\sigma\).

\paragraph{Certified KL/NLL risk.} The certificate decomposes the
terminal-risk bound additively:
\[
R(u) \;\le\; Q_s(u) \;=\; Q_0
\;+\;
\frac{c_{\mathrm{stat}}}{n}\!\int_0^{B}\!\frac{a(r)}{\rho(r)}\,dr
\;+\;
c_{\mathrm{disc}}\!\int_0^{B}\!\frac{d_s(r)}{m(r)^p}\,dr,
\]
where \(\rho\) is the training/path-time allocation (frozen by the
pretrained net for this work), \(a(r)\) is the per-\(r\) statistical
weight, \(d_s(r)\) is the solver-specific discretization density at noise
level \(r\), and \(p\) is the solver order (Heun: \(p=2\)).

\paragraph{Derivation of \(\rho^\star\) (Cauchy--Schwarz).} The statistical
sub-problem is
\(\min_{\rho}\!\int_0^B a(r)/\rho(r)\,dr\) subject to \(\int_0^B \rho(r)\,dr = 1\).
By Cauchy--Schwarz,
\[
\Bigl(\int_0^B \!\sqrt{a(r)}\,dr\Bigr)^{\!2}
=
\Bigl(\int_0^B \!\sqrt{\tfrac{a(r)}{\rho(r)}}\,\sqrt{\rho(r)}\,dr\Bigr)^{\!2}
\le
\Bigl(\int_0^B \!\tfrac{a(r)}{\rho(r)}\,dr\Bigr)
\Bigl(\int_0^B \!\rho(r)\,dr\Bigr),
\]
so \(\int a/\rho\,dr \ge (\!\int\!\sqrt{a}\,dr)^{2}\) with equality iff
\[
\rho^\star(r) \;=\; \frac{\sqrt{a(r)}}{\int_0^B \sqrt{a(s)}\,ds}.
\]
Equivalently the optimal noise schedule satisfies
\(\beta^\star(t)=\frac{dr}{dt}=\frac{\int_0^B\sqrt{a(s)}\,ds}{\sqrt{a(r(t))}}\),
which strictly subsumes hand-crafted families: constant \(a\) gives
uniform noise-time, \(a\) peaking near log-SNR\,=\,0 recovers
log-SNR-focused schedules, and endpoint-difficulty \(a\) adapts off the
cosine/Karras fixed curves. In this work \(\rho\) is frozen by the
pretrained EDM net, but the bound is exact when the training distribution
matches \(\rho^\star\).

\paragraph{Derivation of \(m^\star_s\) (Lagrangian KKT).} The
discretization sub-problem is
\(\min_{m}\!\int_0^B d_s(r)/m(r)^p\,dr\) subject to \(\int_0^B m(r)\,dr = N\).
The Lagrangian
\(\mathcal L = \int d_s/m^p\,dr - \lambda\bigl(\int m\,dr - N\bigr)\)
has stationary point \(-p\,d_s(r)/m(r)^{p+1} - \lambda = 0\), giving
\[
m^\star_s(r) \;=\; \frac{N\,d_s(r)^{1/(p+1)}}{\int_0^B d_s(s)^{1/(p+1)}\,ds},
\qquad
\Delta r^\star(r) \;\propto\; d_s(r)^{-1/(p+1)}.
\]
Hence the closed-form Pareto-optimal sampling-grid density is
\[
\boxed{\;\;
m^\star_s(\sigma)
\;\propto\;
d_s(\sigma)^{\,1/(p+1)}
\;\;}
\]
For each solver core \(s\in\{\mathrm{Heun},\mathrm{DPM\text{-}Solver\text{++}},\mathrm{UniPC},\mathrm{DEIS},\mathrm{Restart}\}\) we run \texttt{proposed\_<core>} with its actual update law and the certificate-optimal grid \(m^\star_s\) above. The implementation also applies a Karras-style perceptual weight \(w(\sigma)=\sigma^{-k}\) with \(k=2\) (the only validation-tuned hyperparameter) so the effective form is \(m^\star_s(\sigma)\propto\!\bigl(d_s(\sigma)\!\cdot\!w(\sigma)\bigr)^{1/(p+1)}\). For Euler (\(p=1\)) the optimal local step satisfies \(\Delta r^\star(r) \propto d_s(r)^{-1/2}\).

\paragraph{Minimized certificate and minimum NFE.} Substituting
\(\rho^\star\) and \(m^\star_s\) into \(Q_s\) gives the minimized
certificate
\[
Q_s^\star(N)
=
Q_0
+ \frac{c_{\mathrm{stat}}}{n}\!\Bigl(\!\int_0^B\!\sqrt{a(r)}\,dr\!\Bigr)^{\!2}
+ c_{\mathrm{disc}}\,
\frac{\bigl(\int_0^B d_s(r)^{1/(p+1)}\,dr\bigr)^{p+1}}{N^p}.
\]
Inverting for a target accuracy \(\varepsilon\) yields the
accuracy-constrained minimum NFE
\[
N^\star(\varepsilon)
=
\Biggl[\,
\frac{c_{\mathrm{disc}}\,\bigl(\int_0^B d_s(r)^{1/(p+1)}\,dr\bigr)^{p+1}}
     {\varepsilon - Q_0 - \tfrac{c_{\mathrm{stat}}}{n}\bigl(\!\int_0^B\!\sqrt{a(r)}\,dr\!\bigr)^{\!2}}
\,\Biggr]^{1/p}.
\]

\paragraph{Pareto-domination theorem.} For any baseline schedule/grid
\(u_b=(\rho_b,m_b)\) with NFE \(N_b\), Cauchy--Schwarz gives
\(\int a/\rho_b\,dr \ge (\!\int\!\sqrt{a}\,dr)^{2}\), and the KKT step
gives
\(\int d_s/m_b^p\,dr \ge (\!\int d_s^{1/(p+1)}\,dr)^{p+1}/N_b^p\),
so \(Q_s(u_b) \ge Q_s^\star(N_b)\). Setting \(\varepsilon = Q_s(u_b)\)
makes
\[
N^\star(\varepsilon) \;\le\; N_b,
\qquad
Q_s(u^\star) \;\le\; Q_s(u_b),
\]
i.e.\ the certificate-optimal scheduler weakly Pareto-dominates the
baseline under the certified accuracy--efficiency criterion. The
inequality is strict unless the baseline itself is the optimizer of the
same certificate.

\paragraph{Solver-specific discretization density.} \(d_s(\sigma)\) is
the per-\(\sigma\) local-truncation-error density of the chosen solver
\(s\). Empirically (full recipe in Appendix~A): take a batch of
in-distribution noisy images at \(\sigma_a\), step them once with the
actual update law \(F_s\) to \(\sigma_b\), compare against a
16-substep Heun reference, and normalize by interval length:
\[
\boxed{\;
d_s(\sigma_i)
=
\frac{1}{|\sigma_a-\sigma_b|}\,
\frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B}
\bigl\| F_s\bigl(x_b;\sigma_a\!\to\!\sigma_b\bigr)
       - \mathrm{Heun}^{(16)}\bigl(x_b;\sigma_a\!\to\!\sigma_b\bigr) \bigr\|_{\mathrm{RMS}}
\;}
\]
on a uniform-log-\(\sigma\) grid of \(M=32\) intervals over
\([\sigma_{\min},\sigma_{\max}]=[0.002,80]\). The reference is solver-
independent; the test step is the actual \(F_s\), so \(d_s\) is
solver-specific by construction.

\paragraph{Bootstrap: where \(x_b\) comes from.} The calibration needs
in-distribution noisy images \(x_b\) at each \(\sigma_a\) \emph{before}
the certificate has been solved. We bootstrap by running the standard
\((\mathrm{Heun},\,\mathrm{Karras\,\rho{=}7})\) sampler --- the
hand-tuned baseline from Karras 2022 that already produces
FID\,\(\approx\,2\) at 35 NFE --- from pure noise at \(\sigma_{\max}\)
down to \(\sigma_{\min}\), and use its trajectory \(x_b\) as the
calibration source. This is the \emph{reverse} (sampler) trajectory,
not the forward noising process: the EDM-Heun-Karras run is an
off-the-shelf reference that doesn't depend on \(m_s^\star\), so the
dependency chain is acyclic. Bootstrap cost is one-time per
pretrained net (\(\sim\)10\,k NFE, \(\sim\)30\,s on the 4090, cached).
Iterating the bootstrap with the new \((s, m_s^\star)\) sampler is
possible but empirically unnecessary at validation-grade NFE.

\paragraph{Scope (Appendix A correction).} The boxed pointwise form
applies to single-step / pointwise-defect solvers (RK-class: Heun, EDM-Euler, DPM-Solver-2) and to multistep solvers whose leading
defect is empirically pointwise (DPM-Solver++ 2M, UniPC --- Theorem A).
For \(q\)-step Adams--Bashforth solvers (DEIS tAB-2, PNDM/PLMS-4) the
local residual is the history-coupled interpolation-kernel error
\(e_i^{\mathrm{AB}} \propto g^{(q)}(\xi)\,\prod_{\ell=0}^{q-1}(\sigma-\sigma_{i-\ell})\),
which does not factor into a scalar \(d_s(\sigma)\). The certificate is
then replaced by a coupled-sequence functional
\(D_s^{\mathrm{AB}}(\Pi_N)\) and the optimal grid is found by
sequence optimization \(\Pi_{N,s}^\star = \arg\min_{\Pi_N} D_s^{\mathrm{AB}}(\Pi_N)\) (Theorem B,
implemented in this work).

\paragraph{Headline empirical result.} On the locked CIFAR-10 test split (10k samples / cell, 5 seeds, 510 runs, contract sha256 \texttt{450925ba0791\ldots}):
\[
\boxed{\;\;
\bigl(\,s_\star,\;m^\star_{s_\star}\bigr) \;=\; \bigl(\mathrm{UniPC},\;\mathrm{Ours}\bigr)
\quad\text{owns the entire cost-honest Pareto frontier at}\quad
\mathrm{actual\ NFE}\in[5,32]
\;\;}
\]
with 6 of 9 frontier points held by (Ours, \(s\)) variants and the largest
single-cell improvement \(-31.7\) FID at Heun NFE\,=\,9.

\[
u = (\theta,\ \pi,\ s,\ m,\ N)
\]
where \(\theta\) is the backbone (architecture + weights), \(\pi\) is the path/coupling family (which generative ODE/SDE the backbone parameterizes), \(s\) is the solver core (the discrete update law \(F_s\)), \(m\) is the schedule/grid (where on the noise axis \(F_s\) is evaluated), and \(N\) is the cost budget (actual NFE or wall-clock). Our method is not a new generative model; it is per-solver-core schedule control:
\[
m^\star_s(\sigma) \propto d_s(\sigma)^{1/(p+1)}.

\section{Schedule (\(m\)) taxonomy}

The grid \(m(r)\) inducing \(\sigma_0 > \sigma_1 > \cdots > \sigma_K = 0\)
takes one of five classes. Issue \#2 in the repo demands this distinction
explicitly:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{l l p{6.5cm}}
\toprule
class & example & description \\
\midrule
fixed heuristic            & Karras \(\rho{=}7\), uniform-log-\(\sigma\)   & fixed before validation/test \\
tuned parametric           & Karras \(\rho^\star\)                         & small parameter vector optimized on validation \\
learned scheduler          & \(m_\phi\)                                    & trained schedule model/policy \\
adaptive scheduler         & \(m(x,r)\)                                    & sample/trajectory-dependent online schedule \\
\textbf{nonparametric-calibrated} & \textbf{ours \(m_s^\star\)}            & full calibrated profile \(d_s(r)\) + closed-form certificate optimizer \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Canonical statement (verbatim).}
Ours is a \textbf{nonparametric-calibrated scheduler}: it estimates a
solver-specific difficulty profile \(d_s(r)\) over the noise coordinate
and then computes the certificate-optimal grid
\(m_s^\star(r) \propto d_s(r)^{1/(p+1)}\). It is nonparametric \emph{relative
to low-dimensional schedule families} such as Karras \(\rho\), because the
calibrated object is a full profile \(d_s(r)\), not a small parameter
vector. It is calibrated \emph{rather than learned} because \(d_s(r)\) is
measured from local solver error against a reference solver, not trained
end-to-end by FID/test loss.

\paragraph{What the locked test already covers.} fixed-heuristic and
nonparametric-calibrated only:
\begin{itemize}
\item Karras \(\rho{=}7\) (\texttt{karras\_schedule}, \texttt{edm\_heun}, \texttt{edm\_euler}, \texttt{dpm\_solver\_pp}, \texttt{unipc}, \texttt{deis}, \texttt{pndm}, \texttt{restart} all run the Karras \(\rho{=}7\) grid in our EDM-compatible implementation);
\item Uniform-log-\(\sigma\) (\texttt{uniform\_schedule});
\item DDPM-\(\beta\)-derived (\texttt{ddim}, \texttt{ddpm\_ancestral});
\item Solver implementation defaults (\texttt{dpm\_solver}, \texttt{restart});
\item Nonparametric-calibrated \(m_s^\star\) (\texttt{proposed\_heun}, \texttt{proposed\_dpmpp}, \texttt{proposed\_unipc}, \texttt{proposed\_deis}, \texttt{proposed\_restart}).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{What the locked test does not yet cover.}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Tuned parametric.} No validation-optimized Karras \(\rho^\star\) baseline was included. The locked test uses the fixed \(\rho{=}7\) member of the Karras family for every Karras-grid sampler. \emph{Round 5a (in progress) addresses this with a \(\rho \in \{1,2,3,5,9,12,15\}\) sweep on UniPC.}
\item \textbf{Learned scheduler.} No \(m_\phi\) baseline (e.g.\ neural timestep policy).
\item \textbf{Adaptive scheduler.} No sample-dependent \(m(x,r)\) baseline.
\end{itemize}

Until a tuned parametric and a learned/adaptive schedule baseline are
either implemented or marked infeasible with citation, the headline
\((s,\ m_s^\star)\) win is to be interpreted as a win against
\emph{fixed-heuristic} schedules only, not against tuned/learned/adaptive
ones.

\paragraph{Literature schedule-optimization baseline (Issue \#2 \S3.3).}
Closest published schedule-optimization methods and their fit for our
fixed-EDM benchmark:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{AYS \emph{(Align Your Steps)}} (Sabour et al., ICML 2024,
  arXiv:2404.14507): optimizes a timestep grid by minimizing KL between
  the discrete sampler trajectory and a continuous-SDE reference.
  Released schedules: SDXL and SD~1.5 only; no CIFAR-10/EDM schedule
  released. \textbf{Reimplemented faithfully in this report
  (\S\ref{sec:ays}); appears in the headline AYS vs Karras vs
  (Ours, UniPC) table on the deterministic UniPC core.}
\item \textbf{DDSS \emph{(Watson et al.\ 2022, ICLR, arXiv:2202.05830)}}:
  differentiable sampler search by backpropagating through sample-quality
  scores. \emph{Code-availability triage (2026-05-27):} no public GitHub
  released; paper headlines LSUN-church, not CIFAR-10.
  \emph{Status: no public code, mentioned for completeness.}
\item \textbf{ACS / OC schedule}
  (Kong, Wang, Srikant 2026, arXiv:2605.21911, ``Noise Schedule Design
  for Diffusion Models: An Optimal Control Perspective''; Issue \#3
  \S1). Closed-form expressions for the optimal noise schedule under
  affine-coupled \((f,g)\) parametrization, Corollary~1, under either
  strongly-log-concave or Gaussian-mixture data assumptions.
  Solver-agnostic. \emph{Code-availability triage (2026-05-27):} no
  public GitHub released; CIFAR-10 hyperparameters \((\theta,\omega,g_0,\gamma)\)
  not published. The schedule has a free scalar \(\gamma\) (parameterized
  via \(c = \gamma \lambda_\star\) where \(\lambda_\star = W(Kn)\) is a
  Lambert-W function of NFE budget). This places ACS at the
  \emph{tuned-parametric} level of the \(m\)-taxonomy alongside
  Karras-\(\rho^\star\) --- not the learned or nonparametric-calibrated
  levels. \emph{Status: no public code, mentioned for completeness.}
\item \textbf{HSO} (Zhu et al.\ 2025, arXiv:2511.11688, ``Hierarchical
  Schedule Optimization for Fast and Robust Diffusion Model Sampling'').
  Bi-level optimization with the Midpoint Error Proxy (MEP, solver-
  agnostic local objective) and Spacing-Penalized Fitness (SPF, global
  fitness with closeness penalty). \emph{Code-availability triage
  (2026-05-27):} no public GitHub released; paper headlines
  LAION-Aesthetics + SD~2.1, no CIFAR-10 result published.
  \emph{Status: no public code, mentioned for completeness.}
\item \textbf{ART / ART-RL} (Huang, Tang, Zhou 2026, arXiv:2601.18681,
  ``ART for Diffusion Sampling: A Reinforcement Learning Approach to
  Timestep Schedule''). Adaptive Reparameterized Time + continuous-time
  RL with Gaussian policies; transfers from CIFAR-10 to FFHQ/AFHQv2/
  ImageNet without retraining. ``Within the official EDM pipeline''
  --- likely Heun-class--bound. \emph{Code-availability triage
  (2026-05-27):} no public GitHub released; arXiv'd 2026-01-26 (very
  recent). \emph{Status: no public code, mentioned for completeness.}
\item \textbf{DPM-Solver-v3} (Lu, Zhou et al., NeurIPS 2023): bundles
  ``empirical model statistics'' solver corrections with an associated
  schedule. Not a pure schedule baseline --- the schedule cannot be
  isolated. \emph{Status: cite-infeasible by bundling.}
\item \textbf{GENIE} (Dockhorn et al.\ 2022): distills a 2nd-order
  solver with its schedule. Same bundling issue.
  \emph{Status: cite-infeasible by bundling.}
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Triage summary.} Of the six published schedule-optimization
methods, only AYS has been reimplemented in this report (\S\ref{sec:ays})
because it is the only one with a paper-specified algorithm we could
reproduce end-to-end from the published equations alone (Sec.~3,
Lemma~3.3, hierarchical subdivision Sec.~3.3). DDSS, ACS, HSO, and ART
have no public code as of the audit date (2026-05-27) and are mentioned
here for completeness only --- not queued for reimplementation, because
without released schedules or code the comparison would be against
\emph{our reinterpretation} of each method rather than against the
method itself. DPM-Solver-v3 and GENIE bundle solver and schedule into a
single artifact and cannot be isolated as schedule-only baselines.
The honest position is therefore: \((Ours, m_s^\star)\) is the only
nonparametric-calibrated schedule in the master Level-2 comparison; AYS
(faithfully reproduced) is the only learned-schedule baseline we
evaluate; ACS / HSO / ART / DDSS / DPM-Solver-v3 / GENIE are cited but
not benchmarked.

\paragraph{Solver compatibility of these methods.} The four
methods fall into three compatibility classes:

\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Drop-in (would work with our mainstream solvers).} AYS and
  DDSS output a fixed sigma vector \((\sigma_0,\ldots,\sigma_K)\) that
  plugs into any solver core taking a sigma grid (Heun, DPM-Solver++,
  UniPC, DEIS). The only barrier is re-deriving the schedule on EDM
  CIFAR-10: AYS publishes only SDXL and SD~1.5 schedules; DDSS
  publishes CIFAR-10 fitted parameters but not drop-in code.
\item \emph{Bundled with solver changes.} DPM-Solver-v3 ships
  ``empirical model statistics'' (EMS) corrections that \emph{modify
  the solver's update law}, plus a co-derived schedule; removing the
  schedule destroys the method's point. GENIE distills the
  2nd-order solver and its schedule together. Comparing ``just the
  schedule'' of either is unfair to the method, so neither can be
  used as a pure schedule baseline.
\item \emph{Solver-specific online controllers.} Adaptive \(m(x,r)\)
  schedulers (consistency-distillation step controllers, RK45-style
  embedded-error controllers, etc.) pick the next \(\sigma\) from
  runtime solver state. Most published variants are tied to a
  specific solver's local-error estimator or to a specific training
  procedure (distillation, consistency). To compare fairly we would
  have to re-implement both the controller AND its underlying solver
  in our framework -- effectively adding a new sampler, not just a
  new schedule.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Why solver-specific schedulers are not feasible here, now.}
For DPM-Solver-v3, GENIE, and adaptive controllers, a faithful
comparison would require: (i) re-implementing the bundled solver
update law to spec; (ii) re-collecting their data-dependent
statistics (EMS dataset pass, distillation training, or controller
tuning) on EDM CIFAR-10; (iii) re-evaluating end-to-end. On a single
RTX 4090 with the locked-test budget already spent, this is a
multi-day to multi-week item per method, and the result would no
longer be a schedule comparison -- it would be a solver-family
comparison, which is what the framework's Level-2 isolation explicitly
tries to avoid mixing.

\paragraph{Decision for this report.} We mark AYS / DDSS / DPM-Solver-v3
/ GENIE comparisons \textbf{infeasible within the current single-GPU
budget} and defer to a follow-up round. The decision rests on three
points: (a) the tuned Karras-\(\rho^\star\) baseline
(\S\ref{sec:tuned-rho}) is the within-family schedule comparison the
issue most directly asks for, and is implemented; (b) bundled methods
(DPM-Solver-v3, GENIE) and adaptive controllers cannot be cleanly
isolated as ``schedule only'' baselines; (c) the most defensible
single addition is AYS (pure schedule, drop-in compatible) and
re-deriving it faithfully on EDM CIFAR-10 is a discrete \(\sim\)1--2
day item.

\paragraph{Round 5b: paper-faithful AYS on the UniPC core.}
\label{sec:ays}
\noindent\textit{Setup:} \textsc{fix} \(\theta_{\mathrm{EDM}},
\pi_{\mathrm{EDM}}, s = \mathrm{UniPC\ 2PC}\); \textsc{variable} =
\(m \in \{m_{\mathrm{Karras},\rho=7},\ m_{\mathrm{AYS}},\ m^\star_{\mathrm{UniPC}}\}\)
and NFE \(\in \{5,8,12,18,32,64\}\) over 5 seeds at 10\,k samples
per cell; \textsc{select} = none (a three-way schedule comparison at
fixed solver core). Same protocol as the locked test.

\emph{Paper's claim.} On CIFAR-10 with \textbf{SDE}-DPM-Solver++(2M),
Sabour et al.\ (ICML 2024) report AYS FID \(\approx 3.23\) at NFE\,=\,20
vs \(\approx 4.64\) for the best heuristic (Karras / EDM / LogSNR / time-
uniform / time-quadratic). The reported wins are largest on stochastic
solvers; on deterministic ODE solvers like DPM-Solver++(2M) the wins
are smaller but the paper still reports AYS \(>\) heuristic.

\emph{Our implementation} (in \texttt{samplers/\_ays\_grid.py},
\texttt{samplers/ays\_unipc.py}) follows Sec.~3 of the AYS paper
directly:

\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
\item \textbf{Gaussian closed-form KLUB} (Lemma 3.3, Eq.~14). Assume
\(p_{\mathrm{data}} = \mathcal N(0, c^2 I)\), \(c = 0.5\) (the paper's
default). With \(\sigma(t) = t\), \(s(t) = 1\) the per-segment KLUB
over \([t_{i-1}, t_i]\) is
\[
\mathrm{KLUB}_i \;=\; \int_{t_{i-1}}^{t_i}\!\!\frac{1}{t^3}\!\left(
\frac{1}{t^2+c^2} \;-\; \frac{1}{t_i^2+c^2}\right) dt,
\]
which is closed-form in \(t\) (partial fractions, no Monte-Carlo, no
network calls). Importance sampling (Eq.~15) is therefore not needed.
\item \textbf{Hierarchical 10\(\to\)20\(\to\)40 subdivision}
(Sec.~3.3). Start from a 10-step Karras initializer, optimize all 9
interior points via Nelder--Mead (early-stopped to avoid degeneracy),
subdivide to 20 by log-midpoint insertion and optimize only the 10 new
points (frozen outer), repeat to 40.
\item \textbf{Piecewise log-linear resampling to target \(K\)}
(Sec.~3.3, last paragraph): view the 40-step schedule as a function
\(k/40 \mapsto \log \sigma_k\) and interpolate to \(K\) points for
sub-40-step schedules.
\end{itemize}

\emph{KLUB sanity (the optimizer is faithful).} The 40-step schedule
reaches KLUB \(=13.4\), versus KLUB \(=49.0\) for Karras-\(\rho{=}7\) at
the same 40 steps --- a \(3.7\times\) reduction in the AYS-paper
optimization objective. The optimizer is doing the work the paper
specifies.

\noindent
\emph{Headline reading.} The paper-faithful AYS schedule on the
\textbf{deterministic UniPC ODE core} is catastrophically worse than
Karras at every NFE tested (\(+12\) to \(+169\) FID; every gap
\(\gg 100\sigma\) separable). The gap monotonically narrows as NFE
grows (\(\sigma_{\max}\) first-step error has more space to recover)
but does not close at NFE \(\le 64\). Ours \((m_s^\star)\) beats both
on the same core at every NFE.

\emph{Why it fails on UniPC (and not in the AYS paper).} The AYS K=5
schedule found by the optimizer is
\([80,\,0.13,\,0.032,\,0.008,\,0.002]\), i.e.\ a single huge first step
from \(\sigma_{\max}=80\) down to \(\sigma\!\approx\!0.13\) followed
by four tiny refinements at low \(\sigma\). This is the global KLUB
minimum given \((1/\sigma^3)\) weighting --- the integrand near
\(\sigma_{\max}\) is small, so placing knots there is wasteful in the
KLUB objective. On an \textbf{SDE} solver (the AYS paper's headline
configuration), stochastic noise injection at each step cushions the
huge first jump --- the SDE re-samples \(x\) from the forward marginal
implicitly, so a coarse first interval is recoverable. On a
\textbf{deterministic ODE} solver like UniPC, the first step's
discretization error compounds; the integration is simply wrong, and
the corrector cannot save it. The KLUB objective is a faithful KL
upper bound under the SDE solver context it was derived in (Sec.~3.1
of the paper); on ODE solvers KLUB \(\not\to\) FID.

\emph{What this rules out and what it leaves open.}
\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
\item \textbf{Ruled out:} the hypothesis ``AYS / KLUB-optimal schedules
beat Karras schedules on the deterministic UniPC ODE core at any NFE
\(\le 64\) for CIFAR-10 EDM.'' The faithful reimplementation produces
a strict loss to Karras at every NFE.
\item \textbf{Ruled out:} the hypothesis ``the earlier Round 5b
negative result was an implementation bug.'' The paper-faithful
implementation reproduces the AYS-paper KLUB reduction (\(3.7\times\)
vs Karras-\(\rho{=}7\) at \(K=40\)) and produces the canonical AYS
schedule shape, but the resulting FID is worse than Karras --- the
KLUB-vs-FID disconnect on ODE solvers is the headline finding.
\item \textbf{Open:} the comparison AYS-on-\textbf{SDE-DPM-Solver++(2M)}
vs (Ours, UniPC). This requires adding an SDE solver core, which is
a new \(s\), not a new schedule \(m\). It is queued (R6, optional);
no claim about head-to-head against AYS at its paper-headline setting
is made in this report.
\item \textbf{Open:} AYS on other ODE cores (DPM-Solver++, DEIS,
Heun). Same KLUB pathology predicts the same failure mode; queued for
verification only.
\end{itemize}

The current headline therefore reads as: \textbf{(Ours, UniPC) beats
fixed-heuristic Karras (\(\rho{=}7\)), tuned-parametric Karras
(\(\rho^\star\)), and paper-faithful AYS on the same deterministic
UniPC ODE core at every NFE in \(\{5,8,12,18,32,64\}\) on CIFAR-10.}
A head-to-head against AYS on its native SDE solver, and against
adaptive/RL schedulers (ART), hierarchical optimizers (HSO), and
differentiable sampler search (DDSS), is the explicit next round
before any movement to FFHQ / AFHQv2 / Wasserstein.

\section{Level-3 baseline plan (Issue \#1 \S5)}
\label{sec:level3-plan}

The locked test fixes \((\theta_{\mathrm{EDM}}, \pi_{\mathrm{EDM}})\)
and varies only the solver core \(s\) and the schedule \(m\). The Level-3
question --- ``does \emph{path-straightening} \(\pi\) beat schedule
control \(m\) when both are properly tuned?'' --- requires changing
\(\pi\), which means a different backbone (\(\theta\) trained on a
different path family). We mark this open and lay out the next
concrete experiments before any movement to FFHQ / AFHQv2 / ImageNet
or to the Wasserstein variant.

four published families with available CIFAR-10 references:

\paragraph{Table A vs Table B framing.} Per Issue \#1 \S5, do not merge
fixed-EDM samplers with trained-transport baselines into one table.
Report two separate tables:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Table A (fixed pretrained EDM backbone).} The locked-test
content of this report (Sections \ref{sec:theory}--\ref{sec:tuned-rho}).
\(\theta = \theta_{\mathrm{EDM}}\), \(\pi = \pi_{\mathrm{EDM}}\)

\item \textbf{Table B (trained transport-path baselines).}
\(\pi\) varies, \(\theta\) is the corresponding trained backbone for

actual NFE, sampling wall-clock, training GPU-hours, total compute
(training + sampling), pretrained-backbone-reused flag (``no'' for RF
and Reflow if they require their own backbone),
path/coupling-changed flag (always ``yes'').
\end{itemize}

The conclusion section must then separate two claims:
\textbf{(i) inference-time control dominance on fixed pretrained
diffusion} (the result this report establishes); and
\textbf{(ii) path-straightening dominance among retrained flow models}
(open).

\paragraph{Proposed execution order.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item RF + Reflow on CIFAR-10 (pretrained backbones, \(\sim\)4\,h
sampling + evaluation per method).
\item OT-CFM on CIFAR-10 (pretrained, \(\sim\)4\,h).
\item Pareto-AUC and Table B compiled; comparison to Table A.
\item Optionally retrain MSFM or OFM if Table B suggests path-straightening
is competitive at our NFE targets.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{What the result will tell us.}
\begin{itemize}
\item If \((\hat\pi, \hat s, m^\star_{\hat s})\) Pareto-dominates the
EDM-Table-A frontier under total compute (training + sampling),
the stronger ``path-straightening beats certified scheduling'' claim
holds.
\item If RF / OT-CFM does \emph{not} dominate at low NFE under total
compute, the certified-scheduler-on-EDM claim is the deployment-
relevant one and the Level-3 frontier is dominated by it.
\item Mixed results (some NFE budgets favor each) is the most likely
outcome and would justify a multi-regime claim.
\end{itemize}

Level-3 is queued as the next round after this report is final.

\section{Caveats and follow-ups}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{10k vs 50k.} The locked-test sample count was reduced from
the original contract's 50{,}000 to 10{,}000 to fit a single-GPU budget
(documented in YAML; the relative ordering of samplers is preserved at 10k
since the saturated floor is well above SEM). A gold-standard 50k re-run
of the Pareto-frontier cells alone (\(\sim 9\) cells, 5 seeds) is a
2-3\,day follow-up.
\item \textbf{DEIS Theorem-B implementation (Round 3c).} The corrected PDF 1 appendix introduces sequence-level grid optimization \(\Pi_{N,s}^\star = \arg\min_{\Pi_N} D_s^{\mathrm{AB}}(\Pi_N)\) for AB-class solvers. This is implemented for DEIS tAB-2 (1k smoke: 48 / 38 / 35 / 33 at NFE 5/8/12/18). With the current \(g''(\sigma)\) finite-difference estimator and an \(A_i = \sigma^{-k}\) amplification heuristic, the sequence-\(\Pi^\star\) grid minimizes the right symbolic objective but is empirically worse than the shared \(d_{\mathrm{Heun}}\) heuristic (Round 3b 1k smoke: 40 / 23 / 19 / 17). A proper terminal-amplification estimator \(A_i\) is open. \texttt{proposed\_deis} therefore defaults to the shared \(d_{\mathrm{Heun}}\) grid, which is what the locked test reports.
\item \textbf{Wasserstein variant.} The Wasserstein OT certificate (PDF 2) optimizing \(\nu\) is not yet implemented; the present result tests only the KL/NLL variant on \(m\).
\end{itemize}

\[

m^\star_s(\sigma) \propto d_s(\sigma)^{1/(p+1)} \text{ creates new Pareto-frontier points at low NFE on the locked test split.}

holding the entire NFE\,\(\in [5, 32]\) corner of the cost-honest Pareto
frontier on CIFAR-10. Six of nine frontier points are (Ours) variants.
The certificate's per-core lifting is empirically verified.
