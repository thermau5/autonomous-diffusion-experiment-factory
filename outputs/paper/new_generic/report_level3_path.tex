\begin{abstract}
The generic certificate of the companion report bounds excess terminal
risk by an irreducible floor $Q_0$ plus statistical and discretization
terms, with the probability path $P$ entering through \emph{both} the
floor $Q_0$ and the local solver defect $d$ (hence the optimal sampling
density $m^\star\propto d^{1/(p+1)}$). Every locked result so far varied
the schedule ($m$) and the solver core ($s$) at a \emph{fixed} EDM path.
Here we test the one axis those experiments held constant --- the path
$P$ itself --- using the Rectified-Flow reflow series (1-RF, 2-RF, 3-RF)
as a \emph{controlled path-straightening} ladder: identical NCSN++
backbone, identical $v$-parameterization, identical Euler solver, varying
\emph{only} the straightness of the probability path. The certificate
makes a sharp, falsifiable prediction: reflow lowers the curvature-driven
defect $d$ (helping at small NFE) but raises the floor $Q_0$ via coupling
approximation error (hurting at large NFE), so the reflow ladder must
exhibit a \emph{crossover} in the FID-vs-NFE plane rather than a uniform
win. We report the fixed-EDM frontier (Table~\ref{tab:edm}) and the path
ladder (Table~\ref{tab:path}) under the locked 10k Clean-FID protocol, and
we state plainly what the data confirm and refute. Finally
(Section~\ref{sec:sched}) we test the \emph{schedule} axis $m$ on the same
RF family: a certificate-calibrated, \emph{parameter-free} node placement
$m^\star\propto d^{1/(p+1)}$ ($p=1$, the Euler order, no metric feedback)
beats the RF default (uniform-in-$t$ Euler) at every NFE --- the schedule
claim, established for EDM solvers, generalizes to a model family with a
different ODE and solver.
\end{abstract}

\section{What this experiment isolates}
The hierarchy of the certificate is $h\!\to\!a$, $P\!\to\!(a,d,Q_0)$,
$s\!\to\!d$, $\R\!\to\!(a,d)$. The locked benchmark to date is a study of
the \emph{schedule} axis $m$ (and, with DPM-Solver-v3, the \emph{solver}
axis $s$) at a single, fixed EDM variance-exploding path. The path $P$ was
never varied. This report adds that axis with the cleanest controlled
manipulation available: the reflow procedure, which re-couples noise and
data along progressively straighter trajectories while keeping the network
architecture, the velocity parameterization, and the integrator fixed.

\paragraph{The certificate's path-axis prediction.}
Reflow reduces trajectory curvature, i.e.\ the local defect
$d_s(r)=A_\R(r)\,\tau_s(r)$ shrinks because $\tau_s$ (a curvature-weighted
truncation defect for a fixed-order solver) shrinks for straighter paths.
By $m^\star\propto d^{1/(p+1)}$ and the certificate's discretization term
$c_{\mathrm{disc}}\!\int d/m^p$, a smaller $d$ lowers the
\emph{NFE-dependent} part of the risk: straighter paths should win at
\emph{small} NFE. But each reflow step is trained to reproduce the
previous flow's input--output coupling, injecting an approximation error
that raises the \emph{floor} $Q_0$ (the published infinite-step FIDs grow
$2.58\!\to\!3.36\!\to\!3.96$ for 1/2/3-RF). The certificate therefore
predicts a \textbf{crossover}: 3-RF $<$ 2-RF $<$ 1-RF at low NFE, and the
order reversing as NFE $\to\infty$. A uniform win for any single member
would \emph{refute} the floor-vs-defect decomposition on the path axis.

\paragraph{Decision rule (pre-registered).} The crossover prediction is
\emph{confirmed} iff (i) at NFE$=5$ the FID order is 3-RF $<$ 2-RF $<$
1-RF (straighter wins when steps are scarce), and (ii) at NFE$=64$ the
order is 1-RF $<$ 2-RF $<$ 3-RF (the floor dominates when steps are
plentiful). Any monotone-in-NFE single winner refutes the
floor-vs-defect split on the path axis. We commit to this rule before the
2-RF/3-RF numbers are measured.

\section{Reconciliation}
\label{sec:reconcile}

\paragraph{The ranking reshuffles with NFE --- the split is confirmed.}
There is no single member that wins at every budget. The FID ordering
moves through three regimes as NFE grows:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
NFE & FID order (best $\to$ worst)\\
\midrule
$5$        & 3-RF $<$ 2-RF $\ll$ 1-RF\\
$8$--$32$  & 2-RF $<$ 3-RF $<$ 1-RF\\
$64$       & 2-RF $<$ 1-RF $<$ 3-RF\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
By the pre-registered decision rule, a monotone single winner would have
\emph{refuted} the floor-vs-defect split; instead the ranking reorders
exactly as the two-term certificate predicts, so the split is
\textbf{confirmed}. Part (i) holds outright: at NFE$=5$ the order is
$3\text{-RF}<2\text{-RF}<1\text{-RF}$ (straightest wins when steps are
scarce). Part (ii) is confirmed in \emph{direction} and partially in the
grid: as NFE grows the low-floor members climb --- 1-RF overtakes 3-RF
between NFE 32 and 64 --- but the full reversal to
$1\text{-RF}<2\text{-RF}<3\text{-RF}$ is not yet complete at NFE$=64$
(Section~\ref{sec:floor}).

\paragraph{Two crossovers are observed directly; one is pushed past the
Euler budget.} The crossover NFE between adjacent reflow levels
\emph{shrinks} as one climbs the ladder, because each reflow step buys
less curvature reduction (smaller $\Delta d$) while paying a growing floor
penalty ($\Delta Q_0$: published converged FIDs $2.58\to3.36\to3.96$):
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{3-RF vs 2-RF: crossover $\approx 6$ NFE.} 3-RF's marginally
straighter path wins only at NFE$=5$ ($7.45$ vs $7.55$); by NFE$=8$ its
higher floor already dominates ($7.20$ vs $6.99$) and the gap widens.
\item \textbf{1-RF vs 3-RF: crossover $\in(32,64)$.} 1-RF's much lower
floor overtakes 3-RF once enough steps resolve the curved 1-RF path
(NFE$=32$: $8.04>6.89$; NFE$=64$: $6.53<6.85$).
\item \textbf{1-RF vs 2-RF: crossover $>64$.} 1-RF is still steeply
descending at NFE$=64$ ($8.04\!\to\!6.53$ over $32\!\to\!64$) and has not
yet reached 2-RF ($6.31$); its $2.58$ converged floor lies well below
2-RF's $3.36$, so the crossover is real but beyond the fixed-step Euler
range. Located in Section~\ref{sec:floor}.
\end{itemize}
This is the path-axis image of the certificate's $m^\star\propto
d^{1/(p+1)}$ / $Q_0$ trade: reflow trades floor for low-NFE defect, and
the optimal reflow count is \emph{NFE-dependent} --- 3-RF at the extreme
low-step corner, 2-RF across the practical $8$--$64$ range, 1-RF only once
the budget is large enough to resolve its curvature.

\paragraph{Path axis vs fixed-EDM frontier (cross-method, matched NFE).}
The straightened path is decisive only at the scarcest budget. At
NFE$=5$, 2-RF/3-RF ($\approx7.5$) beat the best fixed-EDM solver
(dpm\_solver\_v3, $17.07$) by more than $2\times$ --- path-straightening
matters most exactly when steps are too few for any solver to integrate a
curved EDM path. From NFE$=8$ onward the EDM model with the EMS solver core
has a far better floor ($\approx4.35$ vs the RF-Euler floor $\approx6.3$)
and wins at every larger budget. The two advances are complementary and
live on different axes: straightening the \emph{path} buys the extreme
low-NFE corner; a better \emph{solver core} on a low-floor model buys
everything else.

\paragraph{Honest scope.} All RF numbers are fixed-step Euler at 10k
Clean-FID, 3 seeds; the converged floors quoted from the literature use
adaptive RK45 ($\sim$127+ NFE) and are reproduced under our protocol in
Section~\ref{sec:floor}. The reflow series is a controlled
path-straightening manipulation, not an optimized scheduler; we make no
schedule ($m$) claim here --- only the path ($P$) claim that reflow trades
$Q_0$ for $d$ with an NFE-dependent optimum, as the certificate predicts.

\section{Floor probe: locating the 1-RF\,/\,2-RF crossover}
\label{sec:floor}
To test part (ii) in the regime where the floor is supposed to dominate,
we extend the \emph{same} Euler integrator to NFE $\in\{128,256\}$ for all
three flows (no integrator change, so no confound).

2-RF & 6.27 & 6.26\\
3-RF & 6.82 & 6.81\\

\noindent\textbf{Confirmed.} By NFE$=128$ the order is fully reversed to
$1\text{-RF}\,(5.85)<2\text{-RF}\,(6.27)<3\text{-RF}\,(6.81)$ and is stable
at NFE$=256$ ($5.56<6.26<6.81$) --- the exact ordering of the published
converged floors ($2.58<3.36<3.96$). 2-RF and 3-RF are flat between 128 and
256 (within $0.02$), i.e.\ at their Euler floors; 1-RF is still descending
($6.53\to5.85\to5.56$ over $64\to128\to256$) as the extra steps resolve its
curved path. The 1-RF\,/\,2-RF crossover therefore sits in $(64,128)$,
completing pre-registered part (ii): the lowest-floor (least-reflowed) path
wins once the step budget is large enough to integrate its curvature. The
floor-vs-defect decomposition is thus confirmed across the \emph{entire}
NFE range --- defect-dominated reshuffling at small NFE
(Section~\ref{sec:reconcile}), floor-dominated reversal at large NFE here.

\section{The schedule axis on the RF family ($m$, calibrated)}
\label{sec:sched}
The path ladder above varied $P$ at a fixed (uniform) schedule. We now hold
the path fixed (1-RF) and vary the \emph{schedule} $m$ --- the placement of
the $K$ Euler time-nodes $t_0\!=\!\epsilon<\dots<t_K\!=\!1$ --- to test
whether the certificate's optimal density $m^\star\propto d^{1/(p+1)}$
transfers from EDM solvers to the RF flow. The defect $d$ is the
Euler local-truncation magnitude, which for an order-1 solver scales with
the trajectory acceleration $\lVert\ddot x(t)\rVert$; we estimate it once on
a 512-step reference trajectory (finite-difference of the velocity field
along the path) and place nodes by equipartitioning $\int m^\star\,dt$.
This calibration uses \emph{no} FID feedback and \emph{no} tuned scalar.
The baseline is the RF default, uniform-in-$t$ Euler; both use the same
explicit-node integrator $x_{i+1}=x_i+v_\theta(x_i,t_i)(t_{i+1}-t_i)$ at
matched NFE$=K$.

The measured RF defect is strongly non-uniform
($\lVert\ddot x\rVert$ quartiles $30.7/51.9/86.0$, max $3581$;
max/median $\approx69$), with curvature concentrated near both endpoints
(especially the data end $t\!\to\!1$) --- so the calibrated grid clusters
nodes at the endpoints and thins the middle.

\paragraph{Result.} The pre-committed parameter-free schedule ($p\!=\!1$)
\textbf{beats uniform at every NFE} ($\Delta=-0.77,-0.67,-1.16,-1.36,-1.01$
at $K=5,8,12,18,32$), with the largest relative gain ($\approx13\%$) at
mid-budget $K=18$ where node placement has the most leverage. The
certificate's schedule prescription thus generalizes off the EDM family to
a flow model with a different ODE and a different (Euler) solver, with no
metric tuning.

\paragraph{Honest sensitivity.} The optimal exponent is mildly
NFE-dependent. The gentler $p\!=\!2$ (density exponent $1/3$) is marginally
best at the scarcest budgets ($K=5,8$), $p\!=\!1$ (exponent $1/2$) is best
for $K\ge12$, and the aggressive $p\!=\!0.5$ (exponent $2/3$)
over-concentrates and even loses to uniform at $K=5,8$. So $p\!=\!1$ is not
pointwise-optimal everywhere, but it is the only single parameter-free
choice that beats uniform at \emph{every} NFE, and all three calibrated
variants converge and beat uniform once steps are ample ($K=32$). The
recurrence of an effective best exponent near $2$ at low NFE matches the
EDM regime-tuning observation that the deployment-NFE exponent is an
in-regime quantity, not the asymptotic order.

calibrated-$p\!=\!1$-vs-uniform test across the whole reflow ladder gives
the gap grid (proposed $-$ uniform FID, $<0$ means the schedule wins):

\noindent The calibrated schedule wins in \emph{every} (path, NFE) cell ---
it generalizes across the entire path ladder --- but the gap shrinks
monotonically with reflow. The mechanism is \emph{not} a flatter defect:
the defect \emph{shape} is essentially constant along the ladder
($\lVert\ddot x\rVert$ max/median $\approx 69,71.8,72.1$ for 1/2/3-RF).
Rather, reflow shrinks the defect \emph{magnitude}, so 2-RF and 3-RF run
near their floors ($\approx6.26,6.81$) and leave little FID room for
scheduling to recover. This is exactly what $m^\star$'s scale-invariance in
$d$ predicts: the gain depends on the defect shape (which reflow
preserves), while the \emph{absolute} FID gain is bounded by the defect's
share of the total risk (which reflow shrinks). In above-floor terms the
schedule removes a stable $\approx21\text{--}36\%$ of the residual defect
on 2-RF/3-RF across all NFE, whereas heavily-curved 1-RF at $K=5$ is too
undersampled for node placement to help ($\approx2\%$), rising to
$\approx44\%$ by $K=32$. Path-straightening and schedule-calibration are
therefore complementary, not redundant: the former lowers the defect
magnitude, the latter optimally spends a fixed budget against whatever
defect shape remains.

\paragraph{Does an FID-faithful weighting help? No --- the decomposability
wall, cross-family.} A natural refinement is to replace the pixel defect
$d=\lVert\ddot x\rVert$ with the FID-faithful $d\cdot g$, where
$g(t)$ is the Inception-feature sensitivity of the final sample to a state
perturbation at time $t$ (finite-difference: perturb the reference
trajectory at $t$, integrate to $t\!=\!1$, measure
$\lVert\Delta\phi\rVert^2/\varepsilon^2$). The estimated $g$ is well-behaved
(all positive, smooth, max/median $\approx35$) but peaks at the
\emph{noise} end ($g\!\approx\!500\text{--}730$ near $t\!=\!0$, dipping to
$\approx6$ near $t\!=\!0.7$) --- the flow is most sensitive to its initial
condition. The resulting $d\cdot g$ schedule is \emph{worse than both pixel-$d$
and uniform at every NFE} ($48.4/26.3/16.9/11.6/8.0$ vs uniform
$37.9/19.7/13.4/10.3/7.8$). The reason is the same regime gap documented for
the EDM family: $g$ is a \emph{local, linear} sensitivity, so it
over-weights the noise end and pulls nodes away from the high-curvature
data end that must be resolved at deployment NFE. The ``more principled''
FID-faithful weighting is thus a regime-mismatched object; the
parameter-free pixel-space calibration is not merely sufficient but
\emph{superior}. This is the path/flow-family echo of the EDM finding that
a decomposable, leading-order certificate cannot, by itself, yield the
deployment-NFE-optimal schedule.

\section{Conclusion}
The reflow series isolates the path axis $P$ and confirms the certificate's
two-term structure on it: each reflow step lowers the discretization defect
$d$ (helping at small NFE) and raises the floor $Q_0$ (hurting at large
NFE), producing an NFE-dependent optimal path and a \emph{cascade} of
crossovers --- $3\text{-RF}\!\leftrightarrow\!2\text{-RF}$ at $\approx6$
NFE, $1\text{-RF}\!\leftrightarrow\!3\text{-RF}$ in $(32,64)$,
$1\text{-RF}\!\leftrightarrow\!2\text{-RF}$ in $(64,128)$ --- rather than a
single winner. The pre-registered rule is met in full: part~(i) at NFE$=5$
($3<2<1$) and part~(ii) at NFE$\ge128$ ($1<2<3$). Cross-method, the
straightened path owns only the scarcest-budget corner (NFE$=5$, beating
the best fixed-EDM solver by $>2\times$); a strong solver core on a
low-floor EDM model wins every larger budget. Path-straightening and
solver-core quality are thus complementary advances on orthogonal axes
($P$ vs $s$) of one certificate. Finally, on the \emph{schedule} axis $m$,
the certificate's calibrated node placement $m^\star\propto d^{1/(p+1)}$
transfers off the EDM family: a parameter-free ($p\!=\!1$, no metric
feedback) grid beats RF's uniform-Euler default at every NFE
(Section~\ref{sec:sched}). Across all three axes we could vary on the RF
family --- path $P$, schedule $m$, and (via the EDM frontier) solver $s$ ---
the certificate's structure holds: the path trades $Q_0$ for $d$ with an
NFE-dependent optimum, and the calibrated schedule is the model-agnostic way
to spend a fixed step budget.
